Python · Excel · YouTube Analytics

From Views to Learning

Rethinking how NTU's OpenCourseWare measures whether a course video actually works.

Data Source

NTU OCW YouTube Analytics

Role

Data Analysis Assistant

Tools

Python · Excel · YouTube Analytics

Scope

100+ Videos · 36M+ Views

Context

The Problem with View Count

NTU's OpenCourseWare channel has 36M+ cumulative views, but the academic office judged each course video almost entirely by view count. That's a vanity metric: many viewers click the first episode and drop off within the first 1%, so a high-view video isn't one people actually learn from. I set out to define a better measure of an "effective" course video — and turn it into content decisions.

Approach

Moving Past View Count

Using Python and Excel on YouTube Analytics across 100+ videos, I moved past view count to engagement metrics — retention at the 1% / 50% / 70% / 90% marks, completion rate, average view duration and percentage — then segmented behavior by course type and audience. The goal was to understand not just who clicked, but who stayed and learned.

Key Findings

What the Data Shows

01

View count overstates impact

Viewing drops sharply after the first 1% mark, and the 50–90% retention curves nearly converge. "Most-viewed" videos are mostly first episodes people sampled, not finished — high view counts signal discovery, not learning.

02

Course type drives very different viewing behavior

Humanities courses (~2-hour, sequential) shed viewers fast then stabilize to a small loyal core by episode 5. STEM courses (~50-min, topic-based) show selective, unit-by-unit viewing with higher per-episode completion — two completely different engagement patterns requiring different content strategies.

03

Audiences differ sharply by subject

Humanities skews significantly older (55–64 the largest age band, 70% male); STEM skews young (76% aged 18–24, 83% male). These are essentially two different products for two different users — content design and distribution should reflect that.

04

Playlists are an underused growth lever

Playlists account for 30–46% of views — a major discovery path that operates independently of the algorithm. Optimizing playlist structure is a high-leverage, low-cost intervention that current content decisions largely ignore.

Recommendation

Measure What Actually Matters

1 — Replace view count with an engagement-based scorecard

Adopt completion rate, retention at key marks (50% / 90%), and average view percentage as the primary measures of video effectiveness. Raw view count should be a secondary signal for discovery reach, not a quality indicator. This reframes what a "successful" video looks like internally.

2 — Design content to match course-type behavior

Humanities: segment long sequential lectures into shorter standalone units to reduce early drop-off and serve the older, committed audience that already self-selects for depth. STEM: lean into the topic-based structure that already works — viewers are coming for specific units, not cover-to-cover completion.

3 — Invest in playlist structure as a discovery channel

With 30–46% of views originating from playlists, intentional playlist curation and sequencing is a measurable growth lever. Treat playlists as a product surface, not an afterthought — ordered series, curated topic bundles, and cross-course playlists can materially improve both reach and retention.

Methodology & Role

How the Analysis Was Done

Data was exported from the NTU OCW YouTube Analytics dashboard covering 100+ course videos. Python was used to clean, merge, and compute derived metrics (completion rate, retention at threshold marks, average view duration %). Excel was used for segmentation, pivot analysis, and visualization prototyping.

Retention curves were constructed per video by pulling audience-retention data at the 1%, 50%, 70%, and 90% playback marks. Videos were then grouped by course type (humanities vs. STEM) and audience demographic bands to surface behavioral differences. The insights directly informed content-design recommendations delivered to the NTU OCW academic team — including the proposal to retire view count as a primary KPI and replace it with the engagement scorecard.
